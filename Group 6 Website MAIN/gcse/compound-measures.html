Understanding Compound Measures

In this lesson,we will explore the concenpt of Compound Measures in mathematics which includes:

Speed, Density and Pressure

What Are Compound Measures?

Compound measures are calculations made by combining two different measurements.And also are measurements that combine two different quantities to create a new value. They are commonly used in mathematics, science and everyday life.

The most common compound measures at GCSE level are:

  • Speed = Distance ÷ Time
  • Density = Mass ÷ Volume
  • Pressure = Force ÷ Area

What Is It?

Speed measures how fast something moves.

Density measures how compact a material is.

Pressure measures how force is spread over an area.

They are called "compound" because they combine two quantities into one new measurement.

Formula Box

Speed = Distance ÷ Time

Density = Mass ÷ Volume

Pressure = Force ÷ Area

Worked Examples

Example 1: A car travels 120 km in 2 hours.

Speed = 120 ÷ 2 = 60 km/h

Example 2: Mass = 500g, Volume = 100cm³

Density = 500 ÷ 100 = 5 g/cm³

Example 3: Force = 200N, Area = 10m²

Pressure = 200 ÷ 10 = 20 N/m²

Measure What It Compares Example
Speed Distance and Time 60 km in 1 hour = 60 km/h
Density Mass and Volume 500 g ÷ 100 cm³ = 5 g/cm³
Pressure Force and Area 200 N ÷ 10 m² = 20 N/m²

These formulas help us understand how different physical quantities are related. They are widely used in science, engineering and everyday problem solving.

WHAT IS SPEED?

Speed tells us how fast an object moves from one place to another.

It compares the distance travelled with the time taken. If an object travels a large distance in a short time, it has a higher speed.

Speed Formula

Speed = Distance ÷ Time

Distance Time Speed
100 km 2 hours 50 km/h
200 m 20 s 10 m/s
60 km 1 hour 60 km/h

Definition

Speed is a compound measure that tells us how fast something is moving. It measures the distance travelled in a given amount of time.

In simple terms, speed answers the question: “How much distance is covered per unit of time?”

Speed = Distance ÷ Time

Explanation

If an object travels a large distance in a short amount of time, it has a high speed.

If it travels a small distance over a long time, it has a low speed.

Speed can be measured in:

  • m/s (metres per second)
  • km/h (kilometres per hour)

Worked Examples

Example 1

A car travels 150 km in 3 hours. Find the speed.

  1. Formula: Speed = Distance ÷ Time
  2. Substitute: 150 ÷ 3
  3. Calculate: 50
  4. Answer: 50 km/h

Example 2

A runner travels 200 m in 25 seconds. Find the speed.

  1. Speed = 200 ÷ 25
  2. 200 ÷ 25 = 8
  3. Answer: 8 m/s

Example 3

A train travels 360 km in 4 hours. Find the speed.

  1. Speed = 360 ÷ 4
  2. 360 ÷ 4 = 90
  3. Answer: 90 km/h

Example 4

A cyclist travels 45 km in 1.5 hours. Find the speed.

  1. Speed = 45 ÷ 1.5
  2. 45 ÷ 1.5 = 30
  3. Answer: 30 km/h

Example 5

A boat travels 500 m in 50 seconds. Find the speed.

  1. Speed = 500 ÷ 50
  2. 500 ÷ 50 = 10
  3. Answer: 10 m/s

WHAT IS DENSITY?

Density is a compound measure that shows how much mass is contained within a given volume.

It tells us how tightly packed the particles are in a material.

Density measures how compact a material is.

It compares the mass of an object with the volume it occupies. Heavy materials packed into a small space have a higher density.

Density Formula

Density = Mass ÷ Volume

Explanation

If an object has a large mass but small volume, it has a high density.

If it has a small mass but large volume, it has a low density.

Common units include:

  • g/cm³
  • kg/m³
Mass Volume Density
500 g 100 cm³ 5 g/cm³
800 g 200 cm³ 4 g/cm³

Worked Examples

Example 1

Mass = 200g, Volume = 50cm³. Find density.

  1. Formula: Density = Mass ÷ Volume
  2. 200 ÷ 50
  3. = 4
  4. Answer: 4 g/cm³

Example 2

Mass = 500g, Volume = 25cm³.

  1. 500 ÷ 25
  2. = 20
  3. Answer: 20 g/cm³

Example 3

Mass = 900g, Volume = 300cm³.

  1. 900 ÷ 300
  2. = 3
  3. Answer: 3 g/cm³

Example 4

Mass = 1.2kg, Volume = 0.4m³.

  1. 1.2 ÷ 0.4
  2. = 3
  3. Answer: 3 kg/m³

Example 5

Mass = 750g, Volume = 150cm³.

  1. 750 ÷ 150
  2. = 5
  3. Answer: 5 g/cm³

WHAT IS PRESSURE?

Pressure is a compound measure that shows how much force is applied over a given area.

It tells us how concentrated a force is.

Pressure measures how much force is applied over an area.

A larger force acting on a small area produces greater pressure.

Pressure Formula

Pressure = Force ÷ Area

Explanation

If a large force is applied over a small area, the pressure is high.

If the same force is spread over a larger area, the pressure is lower.

Common units:

  • Newtons per metre squared (N/m²)
  • Pascals (Pa)
Force Area Pressure
200 N 10 m² 20 N/m²
500 N 25 m² 20 N/m²

Worked Examples

Example 1

Force = 100N, Area = 20m². Find pressure.

  1. Formula: Pressure = Force ÷ Area
  2. 100 ÷ 20
  3. = 5
  4. Answer: 5 N/m²

Example 2

Force = 300N, Area = 50m².

  1. 300 ÷ 50
  2. = 6
  3. Answer: 6 N/m²

Example 3

Force = 800N, Area = 200m².

  1. 800 ÷ 200
  2. = 4
  3. Answer: 4 N/m²

Example 4

Force = 1,000N, Area = 250m².

  1. 1000 ÷ 250
  2. = 4
  3. Answer: 4 N/m²

Example 5

Force = 900N, Area = 300m².

  1. 900 ÷ 300
  2. = 3
  3. Answer: 3 N/m²
