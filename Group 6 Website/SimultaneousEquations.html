Understanding Simultaneous Equations

In this lesson, we will explore the concept of simultaneous equations in mathematics...

This will be explained through text but if that is not for you under the text is also some videos explaining the subject as well, under the videos you will find some games and lastly a quiz to show how much you have learnt.

Simultaneous Equations Method 1 Elimination

Example: 3x + 7y - 44 = 0 , 2x + 3y = 21

Step 1 - write both equations in the form of ax + by = c if necessary:

3x + 7y = 44

2x + 3y = 21

Step 2 - Manipulate the equations so that the coefficients match - multiplying the equations to make either the x's or y's equal in size (ignoring the signs). Always multiply to get the LCM of the coefficients

(×2) 3x + 7y = 44 ⇒ 6x + 14y = 88

(×3) 2x + 3y = 21 ⇒ 6x + 9y = 63

Step 3 - Add or subtract the equations so one variable cancels out, so that you can find the other variable. In this case, both equations have 6x, so we need to subtract:

(6x + 14y) - (6x + 9y) = 88 - 63

5y = 25

Step 4 - Solve the resulting equations

(÷5) 5y = 25

y = 5

Step 5 - find variable that you eliminated

replace y = 5 into one of the equation and solve to find x:

2x + 3y = 21

2x + 3(5) = 21

2x + 15 = 21

2x = 6

x = 3 and y = 5

Simultaneous Equations Method 2 Substitution

We use the subsititution method when one of the simultaneous equations is quadratic (non-linear)

Example:

Solve -

x2 + 2y = 31

x + y = 14

Step 1: Rearrange the linear equation so that one of the variables is on its own

(−x) x + y ​= 14

y = -x + 14

Step 2: Substitute this variable into the quadratic equation

y = −x + 14 so replace y with −x + 14 −x + 14:

x2 + 2 (-x + 14) = 31

Step 3: Expand and solve to find the values for one variable.

x2 + 2 (-x + 14) = 31

x2 - 2x + 28 = 31

x2 - 2x - 3 = 0

(x - 3)(x + 1) = 0

x = 3 and x = -1

Step 4: Substitute these values back into the linear equation to find y.

when x = 3, y = -3 + 14 = 11

when x = -1, y = -(-1) + 14 = 15

So two solutions exist:

x = 3, y = 11 and x = -1, y = 15

(Note you may need to use the quadratic formula if you get a quadratic equation that is too difficult to solve by factorising)

interpreting Simultaneous Equations Geometrically

To interpret simultaneous equations geometrically, we can graph the equations on a coordinate plane.

The number of solutions is equal to the number of intersections between the graphs:

Two solutions – the graphs intersect twice

One solution – the graphs meet at a single point – the graph is a tangent to the curve at this point

No solutions – the graphs do not intersect
